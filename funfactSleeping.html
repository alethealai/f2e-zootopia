Animal fun fact about SLEEPING

The Standing Sleepers

Zebras often sleep standing up to stay alert for predators. They use a special structure called a stay apparatus — a group of muscles, tendons, and ligaments that allows them to lock their knees. This lets them nap without falling over! However, for deep REM sleep, zebras still need to lie down occasionally.

The Sleepless Giants

In the wild, giraffes can go weeks without sleep — a survival skill to stay alert against predators. When they do rest, it’s often standing up, so they can escape quickly if needed. Amazingly, giraffes only sleep for about 30 minutes a day, in short five-minute naps. Baby giraffes, though, can sleep lying down, curling their necks so their heads rest on their rumps.

The Hand-Holding Dreamers

When sea otters sleep, they float on their backs at the surface of the water. To avoid drifting apart, they often hold hands — forming adorable “rafts” of otters resting together. Sometimes, they even wrap themselves in seaweed to anchor in place while they snooze. Baby sea otters, called pups, can’t float safely on their own yet, so they sleep on their mother’s belly as she gently drifts on the waves.
